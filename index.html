EcoAssets

EcoAssets is a project that brings together 3 research infrastructures …

Image: Germane Jaws

EcoAssets Atlas of Living Australia • Terrestrial Ecosystem Research Network • Integrated Marine Observing System

…the Atlas of Living Australia, the Terrestrial Ecosystem Research Network, and the Integrated Marine Observing System, to provide data in forms that support environmental reporting, such as the State of the Environment Report.

These 3 research infrastructures collect, manage, and organise environmental data across different categories:

The ALA is Australia’s national biodiversity database, bringing together data from natural history collections, government departments, researchers, community groups, and individuals.

TERN measures key terrestrial ecosystem attributes at hundreds of representative locations and produces ecosystem datasets at a national scale.

IMOS collects data on all aspects of the marine environment, with extensive monitoring activities in coastal and open waters.

Image: Germane Jaws

EcoAssets [Building data linkages for environmental reporting]

Historically, data from these activities have certainly fed into environmental reporting. Notably, the 2016 State of the Environment report referenced particular datasets from these 3 infrastructures, but it was difficult to access the data directly, and there was no opportunity down the track for researchers to evaluate and review the conclusions that were reached using these data.

EcoAssets addresses this by delivering standardised, comparable data from these infrastructures, in a form that can be versioned to provide ongoing indicators over time.

Image: Germane Jaws

EcoAssets: Focus Areas

The project has 3 broad areas of focus…

Image: Lottie Corin

EcoAssets: Focus Areas Improve data pipelines

The first is to improve biodiversity data pipelines among the ALA, TERN, and IMOS. In the past, the process of updating and integrating datasets from TERN and IMOS into the Atlas has not always happened consistently. Improving these processes will ensure that our national views, through the Atlas, of species occurrence, distribution, and abundance are being regularly and consistently updated

Image: Lottie Corin

EcoAssets: Focus Areas Improve data pipelines • Deliver aggregated datasets

The second is to deliver aggregated reference datasets in forms that are suitable for environmental assessment

Image: Lottie Corin

EcoAssets: Focus Areas Improve data pipelines • Deliver aggregated datasets • Support ongoing versioning

And the third is to set up a workflow that supports ongoing delivery of these datasets, in such a way that they can bet versioned over time, so this isn’t just a one-off event

Today, I’ll be focussing on this second area; I’ll give an overview of how we’ve derived these aggregated biodiversity datasets from occurrence data in the ALA.

Including information on the type of record, which the Atlas stores in a basisOfRecord field, also allows us to make inferences about how we collect evidence of an occurrence. For much of the 20th century, this evidence came from museum specimens, whereas more recently, this has changed and bulk of data now comes from human observations, which includes data collected by ecologists, field monitoring programs, and increasingly citizen science projects

This image shows the proportion of records that are made up by human observations between 1900 and 1970, with IBRA regions shown in shades or yellow and orange, and IMCRA regions in shades of blue and purple. The darker shades represent higher proportions of human observations

This could have implications for users, in terms of deciding what sorts of data to include (or not). For instance, more charismatic species might be over-represented in citizen projects, giving the impression that there’s been a population boom

In addition to some of these examples that I’ve gone through, we’re also working with a team at ABARES to support their data requirements for the 2023 State of the Forests Report, which involves facetting the data such that we can assess levels of biodiversity within the national forest estate

All these datasets are freely available right now, and I hope seeing some of these examples has given you all ideas for potential use cases. They’re available on the EcoAssets website, which can be accessed at ecoassets.org.au

Clicking on the DATA tab will navigate to a list of all the available assets, and clicking on any of these links will bring up a page with the option to download the data, and view the metadata. This includes a brief description of the dataset, and explanation of how we derived the aggregated counts, and information on how to cite these data if you do use them

Upcoming: event-based datasets

The datasets currently available on the website are based on occurrence records in the Atlas, and the next new and exciting thing we’re working on is generating event-based datasets for EcoAssets. IMOS and TERN both collect significant amounts of data at the level of site-based surveys or through species tracking activities, and ALA likewise has extensive datasets from providers like BirdLife, who carry out systematic surveys at regular intervals. We anticipate that these event-based datasets will complement the biodiversity datasets already available, and provide some measure of the extent of monitoring happening across the country.
